why is privacy a right?

anyone who opposes this view has one question in mind: "if you have nothing to hide, why do you need privacy?" to answer this question, a person who is under surveillance cannot show their true self because a mind that knows it is being watched begins to self-censor on its own.

knowing that an observer is evaluating which book you read, which question you searched, which idea you voiced even as a joke, slowly squeezes that person into the boundaries of what is "acceptable." this is a loss of freedom that goes unnoticed from the outside, but is lived from within.

the issue here is not being "guilty," it is being human. because a human has the right to think, to question, to experiment, to make mistakes and sometimes to change their mind and all of these processes take place not under the constant evaluation of an outside eye, but in a safe space of privacy.

without privacy there is no freedom of thought either; because thinking freely first requires being able to think without being watched.

the question "don't you have anything to hide?" is also built on a false assumption: as if privacy exists only to hide bad things. yet privacy also covers your banking information, your health history, your love letters, the things you pray for, your not-yet-matured ideas, the dreams you haven't shared with anyone.

none of these are "crimes" but they all belong to you, and only you have the right to own them. closing the curtains is not a confession of guilt it is simply part of being human.

indeed, looking at history, it is seen that even those who "have nothing to hide" sooner or later turn out to hold a thought that some regime, some power, or some algorithm finds "objectionable." privacy is the last line of defense against an idea that seems innocent today turning into a crime tomorrow. rules can change, powers can change but someone who can protect their privacy can, to some extent, escape becoming a victim of those changes.
